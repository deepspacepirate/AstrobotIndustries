Nuka Cola Quantum

This project was insane. I took the entirety of the quantum mechanics (QM) series on MIT's Open Courseware, read some highly specialized books on carbon quantum dots (CQDs), and read a lot of papers on CQD synthesis. Strictly speaking, only the last on that list was necessary, but after reading the procedures, I was curious why particular reagents were used. Unable to find satisfactory explanations on the internet, I sought out textbooks on quantum dots. Unsurprisingly, learning about quantum dots required familiarity with QM. So began my quest to lean QM.

I learned is not that hard, actually. I encourage you all to learn it!

Research

Prior to the start of this project, I was passingly familiar with some of the materials and methods used to create quantum dots. Not wanting to work with heavy metals in atmospheres of pure argon, I sought out information on CQDs. I wanted a synthesis that required the least amount of specialized equipment, and had the least involved processes. Carbon quantum dots: synthesis, properties and applications was my launching point for this project. Like a good student using the original sources instead of citing Wikipedia directly, I followed the links to the original papers. Fortunately, for a number of reasons, I have student IDs for about 4 colleges/universities, so getting access to them was trivial.

I'm entirely convinced CQD chemists just go through their lunchboxes to find carbonaceous starting material. I've read papers where researchers use watermelon rinds, orange juice, table sugar, quince fruit, mangoes, tomatoes, guava, red pepper, peas and spinach. There's no limit to what you can make CQDs out of, probably. As long as it has carbon, you can do it!
